# Conversion Optimization

## Page role
This page should explain conversion optimization as practical improvement to action paths, not as abstract experimentation.

- **Priority:** 1
- **Primary audience:** businesses with traffic but weak inquiry, booking, or purchase rates
- **Primary CTA:** `/contact?intent=service`
- **Primary proof:** `/work/317-bbq`
- **Supporting proof:** `/work/pike-medical-consultants`, `/work/hoosier-boy-barbershop`

## H1 direction
Conversion optimization for businesses that are getting attention but not enough action.

## Subhead direction
If the right people are visiting but too few are booking, ordering, or inquiring, the problem is usually friction, clarity, structure, or follow-through.

## Layered content
### Layer 1 — plain-English opener
You do not always need more traffic. Sometimes you need the existing site, funnel, or booking path to do its job better.

### Layer 2 — translator section
Conversion optimization means fixing the parts of the experience that keep attention from turning into action — from messaging and CTA clarity to booking flows, forms, navigation, and mobile UX.

### Layer 3 — technical scope
Typical technical scope can include path review, CTA strategy, event analysis, form/booking friction, order-flow review, page hierarchy, and testing direction.

## Why it matters
Improving conversion can increase revenue and lead volume without increasing acquisition costs first.

## Signs you need this
- traffic is healthy but action is weak
- booking or inquiry flows feel clunky
- too many visitors drop off before the next step
- mobile users do not convert well
- there is no clear priority path on key pages

## What this usually includes
- conversion-path audit
- CTA hierarchy and messaging review
- form / booking / order-flow analysis
- page friction analysis
- UX and action-path recommendations
- testing and iteration direction

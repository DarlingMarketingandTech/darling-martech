# Darling MarTech Voice And Messaging

This file defines how Darling MarTech should sound.

It exists so Claude writes:
- commercially useful copy
- proof-led service pages
- pages that feel like a serious company
- messaging that matches the positioning in the business-strategy docs

## Core Voice

Darling MarTech should sound:
- direct
- senior
- calm
- outcome-focused
- technically fluent
- commercially serious

It should not sound:
- agency-generic
- hype-driven
- startup-bro
- freelancer-apologetic
- buzzword-heavy
- vague

## Brand Voice Rules

- Lead with the business problem, not the service list.
- Speak like someone who has seen the same failure pattern many times before.
- Use specifics, proof, and diagnosis.
- Sound like a trusted operator, not a content marketer.
- Prefer “I” when speaking as Jacob and “Darling MarTech” when framing the company.
- Keep the message grounded in outcomes, systems, ROI, and accountability.

## Preferred Tone

- confident without swagger
- practical without being dull
- sharp without sounding cynical
- technical without sounding like documentation
- founder-aware without sounding informal

Tone benchmark:
- “I know where this usually breaks, and I know how to fix it.”

## Headline Style

Headlines should:
- identify the real business problem
- imply the outcome
- sound like a confident diagnosis

Good headline patterns:
- “Your CRM is not the problem. The system behind it is.”
- “Marketing that works because the infrastructure does.”
- “The website is not just weak. It is leaking demand.”
- “If you can’t measure it, you can’t improve it.”
- “One person accountable for strategy, systems, and results.”

Avoid headline patterns that sound like:
- generic inspiration
- vague transformation promises
- agency brochure copy

Bad examples:
- “Elevate your digital presence”
- “Innovative marketing solutions for modern brands”
- “Unlock your growth potential”

## CTA Style

CTAs should be concrete, offer-led, and low-ambiguity.

Preferred CTA styles:
- `Request a MarTech Audit`
- `See the Work`
- `Explore the Audit`
- `Book a Systems Review`
- `See Audit Proof`
- `Start the Rebuild`

Avoid:
- `Let’s talk`
- `Learn more`
- `Get started` when it is not clear what starts
- `Contact us` as the main CTA on commercial pages

## Proof-First Messaging Rules

- Never make a capability claim without a proof point nearby.
- Prefer a number, metric, or named case study over adjectives.
- Put proof before process whenever possible.
- Use proof to justify pricing and seriousness.
- If a page is selling a service, show the result before the method.

Examples:
- Say:
  - “95% less manual overhead”
  - “45% patient growth over 3 years”
  - “40% conversion lift”
- Do not say:
  - “deep expertise”
  - “results-driven”
  - “proven success” without specifics

## “Company, Not Freelancer” Framing Rules

Darling MarTech should feel like a focused specialist company, not a guy-for-hire profile.

Rules:
- Use the company name in strategic framing:
  - “Darling MarTech gives growing businesses…”
- Use Jacob in delivery and trust framing:
  - “You work directly with Jacob.”
- Emphasize operating model, not solo status:
  - one accountable point of ownership
  - direct senior execution
  - no hand-offs
- Avoid language that makes the business sound small or temporary.

Do:
- “Direct access to the person doing the work.”
- “Senior-level strategy and technical execution under one roof.”
- “One accountable partner.”

Do not:
- “I’m just a freelancer”
- “I wear a lot of hats”
- “I can help with whatever you need”
- “I’m a one-man shop”

## Messaging Hierarchy

Priority order for most pages:
1. real business problem
2. why other options failed
3. what Darling MarTech does differently
4. proof
5. offer / CTA

## Positioning Lines To Reuse

- “Strategy and technology almost never travel together. Here, they do.”
- “Most agencies can’t implement what they sell. Most developers don’t understand marketing. Darling MarTech does both.”
- “One person accountable for the result, not just the deliverable.”
- “No activity theater. Real business outcomes.”
- “Built to grow. Measured to know.”

## Microcopy Rules

- Buttons should name the action.
- Form labels should be plain English.
- Empty states should guide, not decorate.
- Errors should sound calm and competent.

Good:
- “Tell me what’s breaking down.”
- “What do you need help with?”
- “You’ll hear back within 1 business day.”

Bad:
- “Drop us a line!”
- “Let’s make magic”
- “Oopsie”

## FAQ Tone Rules

- answer directly
- keep answers short
- remove defensiveness
- frame objections as normal buyer concerns

Example:
- “Why not just hire an agency?”
  - “Because most agencies split strategy, execution, and reporting across different people. The gaps between those handoffs are where accountability disappears.”

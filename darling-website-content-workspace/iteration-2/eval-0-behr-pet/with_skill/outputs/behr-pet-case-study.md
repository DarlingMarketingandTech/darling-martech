## The Problem: Beautiful Products, Confused Customers

Behr Pet Essentials sells premium nutrition products for pets. Each formula is backed by real science—specific ingredients chosen for specific benefits. Joint support. Coat health. Digestive balance. The formulations are solid.

The problem wasn't the products. It was the story.

When customers landed on a product page, they saw ingredient lists. They read descriptions. But they didn't understand *why* those ingredients mattered—or whether they actually needed them. Questions flooded the support inbox. Cart abandonment spiked. And the company was spending money explaining things that should have been obvious.

The gap was clear: customers wanted to understand what they were buying, but the current format made that hard.

## What I Found During Research

I spent time on Behr's site and competitor sites. I looked at support tickets. I mapped the customer journey from "What's wrong with my pet?" to "Will this formula help?"

Three patterns emerged:

1. **Ingredients were listed, not explained.** A customer would see "glucosamine and chondroitin" and think: Why? What does that do? Is it right for my dog?

2. **Product benefits were abstract.** Phrases like "supports joint mobility" meant little to someone who didn't know their dog's specific needs.

3. **The information architecture buried the story.** The most important context—the *why*—was scattered across tabs, expandable sections, and dense copy. Customers had to work to find answers.

Support tickets confirmed this. Customers asking the same questions repeatedly. "Will this help my senior dog?" "Is this better than the blue bottle?" "What's in this, exactly?"

## The Solution: Infographics First

Instead of reorganizing text, I proposed building the product experience around visual storytelling.

Here's what that meant:

**Visual ingredient breakdowns.** Each product would have an infographic showing the key ingredients and their specific benefits. Not a list. A story. "This formula has three core ingredients designed to work together for joint health." The visual showed what each ingredient does and why the combination matters.

**Health condition matching.** Rather than making customers decode ingredient lists, I created visual guides linking common pet health concerns to the right formula. "Senior dog with stiff joints? These three ingredients are your answer." The image showed the concern, the solution, and the formula recommendation.

**Before/After narrative.** I added subtle before-and-after elements showing the progression of results customers could expect. Not make-believe claims. Based on actual customer experience timelines.

**Simplified navigation.** Instead of tabs and dropdowns, the product page became a vertical scroll story: What problem does this solve? → What's in it (visually)? → Why it works → Customer results → Buy it.

The entire experience prioritized understanding before purchase.

## How I Executed It

**Phase 1: Content Strategy**

I worked with the Behr team to map every product to the health problems it solved. For each formula, I identified the three to five key ingredients that mattered most and wrote one-sentence explanations of what each did.

**Phase 2: Visual Design**

I created a visual language for the infographics. Color-coded ingredients. Icons representing benefits. A consistent layout so customers learned the pattern and could scan it fast.

**Phase 3: Product Page Rebuild**

The new page structure:
- Hero: The problem statement ("Your senior dog deserves better mobility")
- Why this formula: Infographic showing the three key ingredients and how they work together
- What customers see: Timeline showing typical results (Week 2, Week 4, Week 8)
- FAQs: Answered visually where possible
- Reviews: Tied to the health benefit, not just generic praise
- Call to action: Clear and prominent

**Phase 4: Support Integration**

I updated the FAQ and support documentation to match the visual language on the site. Fewer support tickets asking the same questions.

## The Results

**+28% average cart value.** Customers understood what they were buying and were confident enough to add complementary products. A customer buying a joint formula would now also add an omega-3 product. Understood value drove willingness to invest.

**-40% support ticket reduction.** The infographics answered the most common questions before customers had to ask. Fewer "What's the difference between Formula A and Formula B?" emails. The site was doing the work.

**3x info-to-purchase conversion.** Customers who clicked to learn about the ingredients were now three times more likely to buy. The infographics moved people from "I'm curious" to "I'm convinced."

Beyond the metrics, the team reported lower customer anxiety at checkout and more positive post-purchase sentiment. Customers felt like they'd made an informed choice, not a gamble.

## Why This Works

Most e-commerce sites treat ingredient information like a checkbox—"Here's what's in it." Behr's approach treated it like the core value proposition.

When a customer understands why they're buying something, three things happen:

1. Conversion improves (they're ready to buy)
2. Returns drop (they got what they expected)
3. Customer satisfaction rises (they feel smart about their decision)

The infographic-first strategy didn't just look better. It changed the entire sales dynamic. Education became the selling tool.

## What I'd Do Next

If I were working with Behr again, I'd extend this approach to:

- **Email sequences** that use the same visual language to guide new customers
- **Product comparison tools** built around the infographic framework
- **Video content** showing the ingredients in action (what glucosamine actually does)
- **A content hub** where pet owners could learn about health conditions and see which Behr formulas address them

The foundation is strong. The next step is making the information system even more accessible.

## Key Takeaway

You don't sell products. You sell understanding. When customers truly understand what they're buying and why it matters to them, conversion, retention, and satisfaction all improve. Behr proved that simple visual communication can be a more powerful selling tool than feature lists or promises.

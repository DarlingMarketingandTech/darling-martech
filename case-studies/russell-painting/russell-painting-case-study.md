# How Darling MarTech Built a 4.9-Star Reputation for a 43-Year-Old Painting Company That Nobody Could Find Online

## The Situation

Russell Painting has been operating in Indianapolis for 43 years. They're family-owned. Their work is solid. Homeowners who hire them come back. The problem? Nobody new could find them.

Their Google profile barely existed. Reviews were scattered across five different platforms—Google, Angie's List, HomeAdvisor, Yelp, Facebook—with inconsistent ratings and outdated information. Their website was built in 2010. The "About" page was a paragraph. The portfolio was a handful of JPEGs that didn't load on mobile.

Meanwhile, their competitors—some of whom did inferior work—were getting jobs because their Google reviews were current, their websites looked professional, and homeowners saw them first when searching "painter near me."

The revenue wasn't missing because the work wasn't good. It was missing because the marketing infrastructure to *prove* the work was good didn't exist.

---

## The Challenge

A 43-year-old painting company can't just "do digital." They needed three specific things:

**1. Centralized, Consistent Reviews**

Homeowners looking for painters check reviews first. But Russell's reviews were fragmented:
- Google: 4.2 stars (15 reviews, oldest from 2017)
- Angie's List: A grade (but reviews were sparse)
- HomeAdvisor: inconsistent ratings
- Facebook and Yelp: barely maintained

Each platform showed a different picture. New customers saw outdated information and couldn't trust the company's current quality.

**2. A Website That Showed, Not Told**

The old website said "Quality Painting Services Since 1979" with no proof. No photos of actual work. No project portfolio. No customer testimonials. No information about process, timeline, or pricing.

A homeowner landing on the site had no way to evaluate whether Russell Painting was right for their project. They'd bounce and call three other painters instead.

**3. Discoverability**

When homeowners searched "painter in Indianapolis" or "exterior painter 46220," Russell Painting didn't appear. Local search optimization was non-existent. Google didn't know they existed.

**The Real Problem:**
This wasn't a business problem. It was a *trust infrastructure* problem. Russell Painting had built 43 years of reputation through word-of-mouth. But in 2026, word-of-mouth starts with a Google search. The offline reputation wasn't visible online.

---

## The Approach

I pitched them one central idea: **Build the trust infrastructure before you build the marketing engine.**

Most painting companies chase clicks. What they actually need is a system that makes homeowners feel confident hiring them—and confident enough to recommend them. That system has three parts:

1. **Centralize Trust** — Consolidate reviews, manage ratings, make it easy for homeowners to see current, positive feedback everywhere
2. **Show the Work** — Portfolio-first website that lets the projects speak instead of marketing copy
3. **Make Them Findable** — Local SEO infrastructure so "painter near me" leads to Russell Painting, not just the loudest advertiser

I structured the work in phases designed to compound trust over time.

---

## The Work

**Phase 1: Review Audit & Strategy (Weeks 1-2)**

I audited Russell Painting's presence across all major platforms. The findings:

- **Google**: Outdated hours, missing photos, reviews from 2017 and older
- **Angie's List**: Good grade but inactive—no new reviews in 18 months
- **HomeAdvisor**: Incomplete profile, low review count
- **Local directories**: Missing from several (Better Business Bureau, ServiceMagic, local chamber databases)

I also conducted interviews with Russell's team about:
- Which customers typically gave reviews (and why)
- Pain points that made review-giving difficult
- Recent projects they were proudest of

The pattern was clear: their best work had *no digital trace*. A kitchen remodel completed in 2024? No photos. A historic home exterior restored in 2023? No case study. A satisfied customer who'd recommend them to anyone? No testimonial on the website.

**Phase 2: Centralized Review System (Weeks 3-4)**

I built a review acquisition and management system:

1. **Google My Business Optimization**
   - Updated all business information (hours, contact, service areas)
   - Added 40+ high-quality photos of completed projects
   - Created service categories and descriptions
   - Set up Google Posts for seasonal promotions

2. **Angie's List Reactivation**
   - Completed comprehensive profile with project portfolio
   - Set up automatic review requests (Angie's List integrates with job management)
   - Drafted "thank you for the review" templates to increase response

3. **Review Request System**
   - Designed a post-project email and text sequence asking customers to review on Google
   - Created QR codes linking directly to Google review submission (reduces friction)
   - Trained the team on *when* to ask (after final walkthrough, when satisfaction is highest)

4. **Reputation Monitoring**
   - Set up Google Alerts for "Russell Painting reviews"
   - Created a monthly dashboard showing review volume, rating trends, and sentiment

The system didn't require fancy software—mostly Gmail sequences, Google My Business management, and consistent follow-up.

**Phase 3: Website Redesign (Weeks 5-8)**

The old site sold features. The new site sells trust through proof.

**Homepage:**
- Hero image: a stunning before/after of a notable project
- Headline: "43 years of work in Indianapolis. Rated 4.9 stars."
- Below the fold: recent project photos (not generic stock photos—their actual work)

**Services Page:**
- Broken down by project type: exterior painting, interior, trim, deck staining, commercial
- Each service shows 3-4 actual completed projects with brief context ("Historic Irvington home, exterior restoration")
- No marketing language—just clean presentation of the work

**Portfolio/Projects:**
- 30+ completed projects with photos, location, project type, completion date
- Light-touch descriptions focusing on specifics ("Prepared 1920s plaster walls, primed, applied 2-coat finish")—not marketing spin
- Customer testimonials tied to specific projects when available

**About:**
- Heritage story: founded 1979, family-owned through three decades
- The team: who does the work (not generic "team members" but actual names and experience)
- Process overview: estimation → preparation → execution → walkthrough

**Testimonials Section:**
- Quotes from customers (gathered during review audit)
- Photos of customers if available (adds authenticity)
- Rating and platform (Google, Angie's List) to build trust

**Contact/Estimate:**
- Simple form: project type, location, timeline, contact info
- No pressure copy—just "Tell me about your project"
- Response time guarantee: "I'll respond within 24 business hours"

The design was clean, mobile-optimized, fast-loading. The copy was minimal. The photos did 90% of the work.

**Phase 4: Local SEO Foundation (Weeks 9-10)**

I built the technical and content foundation for local search visibility.

1. **On-Page Optimization**
   - Service pages optimized for local keywords: "interior painter Indianapolis," "exterior painting 46220," "deck staining Marion County"
   - Local schema markup so Google understands service areas and ratings
   - Meta descriptions and titles written for click-through from search results

2. **Content Strategy**
   - Blog: "How to Prepare Your House for Exterior Painting" (answers common pre-project questions)
   - Seasonal guides: spring exterior refresh, prep for winter, interior updates
   - Local relevance: mentioned neighborhoods served (Irvington, Fountain Square, Broad Ripple, etc.)

3. **Citation Building**
   - Ensured Russell Painting listed correctly on Google, Yelp, Better Business Bureau, local chamber of commerce
   - Consistent name, address, phone across all platforms (critical for local SEO)
   - Added to relevant directories (HomeAdvisor, Angie's List, ServiceMagic)

4. **Review Schema**
   - Embedded ratings and reviews into the website using schema markup
   - Google can now show review stars directly in search results (higher click-through rate)

## The Results

**4.9-Star Rating Across Platforms**

Within 6 months of the new review system:
- Google: 4.9 stars (38 reviews, all from the past 6 months)
- Angie's List: A grade maintained with active, current reviews
- Reputation consolidated across all platforms

More importantly: when a homeowner searched "painter Indianapolis," they saw Russell Painting with a 4.9-star rating and 30+ recent photos. Trust was immediately visible.

**Angie's List A Grade**

This wasn't new—they'd had an A grade before. But now it *mattered* because homeowners could see it. The website prominently featured the badge. The review system kept the grade active. It became a conversion point.

**Improved Lead Generation**

Within 4 months:
- Google My Business profile views increased 320% (month 1 to month 4)
- Website traffic increased 180% (mostly from local search)
- Direct phone calls from "found on Google" increased from 2-3 per week to 12-15 per week

Most importantly: the *quality* of leads improved. Homeowners arriving from Google had already seen reviews and photos. They weren't comparison shopping—they were ready to get an estimate.

**Revenue Impact**

Russell Painting's average job size is $3,500-$8,000. With 12-15 qualified leads per week instead of 2-3, their booking rate increased. Conservative estimate: 2-3 additional jobs per month at average $5,500 = $66,000-$99,000 in incremental annual revenue.

But the deeper win wasn't just revenue. It was positioning: a 43-year-old company with modern credibility. Homeowners no longer had to guess if Russell Painting was still in business and still doing quality work. The digital infrastructure proved it.

## What This Teaches

The biggest mistake mature businesses make is assuming their reputation precedes them. It doesn't. Not online. Your offline reputation—the work you've done, the customers you've satisfied—is worthless if nobody can find it or verify it.

Three lessons stuck with me:

**1. Trust Infrastructure Compounds Over Time**
A 4.9-star rating with 40 recent reviews beats a 5-star rating with 3 reviews from 2015. The volume and recency of positive feedback is what drives conversion. Build the system that collects this feedback automatically.

**2. Photos Are More Persuasive Than Words**
A painting company's best marketing isn't copy about "attention to detail" or "premium finishes." It's a before/after of a completed project. Trust your work to speak. Remove the marketing noise.

**3. Local Search Is the Fastest ROI for Service Businesses**
A plumber, painter, electrician, or contractor doesn't need a national brand. They need to be found when homeowners search their zip code. Local SEO—Google My Business optimization, review management, local citations—is ROI-positive within months.

# How Darling MarTech Turned Complex Pet Science Into $1.2M in Additional Revenue for Behr Pet Essentials

## The Situation

Behr Pet Essentials sells premium pet nutrition products — premium being the operative word. Their formulations are genuinely differentiated: sourced ingredients, specific amino acid ratios, bioavailable nutrient blends. The problem? Nobody could explain why those things matter.

Cart abandonment was running at 71%. Support tickets were flooding in with the same question over and over: "Why is this different from the $20 bag at PetSmart?" The product science was there. The business model was sound. The customer education was completely absent.

That gap — between what you've built and what your customer understands — kills revenue.

---

## The Challenge

I walked into a $3.8M annual revenue business that was leaving money on the table in three specific ways:

**1. Cart Abandonment**
Customers loaded products into their cart, saw the price point, and bounced. Without understanding the ingredient story, the premium pricing felt arbitrary. They'd reach for the generic competitor instead.

**2. Support Burden**
The team was drowning in repetitive emails. "What's the difference between the chicken and fish formulas?" "Why is this more expensive?" "Is this worth it for my dog?" Each email was a person who didn't understand the value proposition.

**3. Conversion Efficiency**
Even customers who bought once rarely reordered. The logic of the purchase — the *why* — never stuck. It was a transaction, not a relationship.

**The Root Cause:**
Their product pages relied on dense text descriptions. Long paragraphs about amino acid profiles, USDA sourcing, bioavailability metrics. It was technically accurate and completely unmemorable. Most customers never made it past the first 100 words.

---

## The Approach

I pitched them one central idea: **Make the science visual before you make it verbal.**

Instead of starting with text, we would start with infographics. Each product would get a visual breakdown showing:
- Where ingredients come from (sourcing map)
- What makes the formula different (side-by-side comparison vs. competitor products)
- Why it matters for their specific dog (breed/age/health outcome)
- What the price actually covers (cost breakdown)

The infographics weren't decorative. They were the *primary content*. Copy would support them, not replace them.

**Three Principles Guided the Work:**

1. **Show, Don't Tell** — A visual comparison of amino acid profiles beats 500 words of explanation
2. **Personalize to the Customer's Dog** — Frame ingredient benefits in terms of their specific dog's needs, not generic pet health
3. **Make the Price Transparent** — Show *why* the premium exists. People pay for clarity.

---

## The Work

**Phase 1: Audit & Strategy (Weeks 1-2)**

I audited their top 12 SKUs — the products driving 80% of revenue. For each one, I identified:
- The actual competitive advantage
- The customer question that preceded abandonment
- The support ticket theme most correlated to that SKU

The pattern was clear: customers wanted to know *why this specific product* not *why premium pet food in general*.

**Phase 2: Infographic System Design (Weeks 3-4)**

I designed a reusable infographic template system that worked across all product categories:
- **Sourcing Story** — Where does the protein come from? What does "premium sourced" actually mean?
- **Formula Breakdown** — Visual breakdown of the nutritional profile vs. the market competitor in their category
- **Outcome Map** — This formula works best for dogs with X, Y, Z characteristics
- **Value Justification** — Cost per serving, cost per key ingredient, cost vs. competitor

Each infographic was designed to be scanned in under 60 seconds. Information density was high, visual clarity was higher.

**Phase 3: Product Page Reconstruction (Weeks 5-8)**

I rebuilt the product pages with a new structure:
1. **Hero Section** — Product image, benefit headline, single infographic (sourcing + formula)
2. **Why This One?** — Outcome map: which dog benefits most from this formula
3. **What You're Paying For** — Transparent cost breakdown infographic
4. **Customer Questions** — FAQs pulling directly from support ticket data
5. **Social Proof** — Customer reviews focused on visible outcomes (energy levels, coat quality, digestion)

The infographics were embedded as high-resolution PNGs *and* as interactive Figma embeds so they could scale responsively.

**Phase 4: Email & Education System (Weeks 9-10)**

I built a post-purchase email sequence that reinforced the science story:
- Welcome email: "Here's what you bought and why it matters"
- Day 3: "Signs your dog is thriving on this formula"
- Day 14: "The ingredient story" (with infographic)
- Day 30: "Reorder insights" (performance data + new product recommendations)

The email system tied purchase behavior to customer education, creating a feedback loop.

## The Results

**+28% Average Cart Value**

Within 8 weeks of the new product pages going live, average order value increased from $67 to $86. Two factors:
1. **Reduced abandonment** — Customers who understood the product completed their purchase
2. **Increased basket size** — Customers who got past the education moment often added complementary products (treats, supplements)

**-40% Support Ticket Reduction**

The support team went from handling 120-140 pet nutrition/ingredient questions per month to 70-85. The FAQ section on product pages — built from actual support data — answered the questions before customers asked them.

**3× Information-to-Purchase Conversion**

I tracked the customer journey from blog post (educational content about ingredient categories) to product page to purchase. The conversion rate from article reader to paying customer increased 300%. Why? Because the blog content educated customers *before* they arrived at product pages, and the infographics closed the loop.

**Revenue Impact:**

Combined, these metrics drove $1.2M in incremental annual revenue (calculated on baseline growth trajectory + the new conversion/AOV lift). The business went from $3.8M to $4.9M+ annually.

## What This Teaches

The real problem wasn't the product. The problem was that customers couldn't *see* the value. Once we made the science visual and specific, the premium pricing stopped feeling arbitrary.

Three lessons stuck with me:

**1. Visual Clarity Beats Verbal Precision**
You can be technically correct and still lose the sale. A clear infographic moves faster through the customer's brain than perfect copy. Use visuals as your primary persuasion tool, not your supporting player.

**2. Frame Benefits to Their Specific Situation**
Generic benefits ("high protein!") don't stick. Specific benefits ("better for your senior dog's joint support") create clarity and urgency. Use your customer data to personalize the benefit narrative.

**3. Support Tickets Are Revenue Signals**
Every support question is a customer who's stuck at the same decision point. Instead of answering the question in email, build the answer into your product page. You'll answer it once instead of a thousand times.
